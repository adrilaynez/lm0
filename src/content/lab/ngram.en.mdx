<Section id="ngram-01" number="01" label="Looking further back" heading="Looking further back">

In the last chapter we left our machine trapped in a perpetual present. The bigram model could only remember the last character it had read or written. The moment it moved on to the next one, it forgot everything that came before.

That was exactly the source of many of its mistakes: situations that to us are different looked, to the machine, exactly the same.

<Lead>How can we give it a little more context? The simplest idea is to let it look a little further back. Does that change its bets?</Lead>

<Figure label="give it a little more memory"><WidenWindow /></Figure>

A little more context, and things change quite a bit.

From now on, so we don't keep saying "a two-letter window" or "a three-letter window", we'll call that window size **N**. Our old bigram model was, simply, a model with **N=1**. Let's see how to build one with **N=2**.
</Section>

<Break />

<Section id="ngram-02" number="02" label="Boxes within boxes" heading="Boxes within boxes">

If we want to give the machine a two-character memory, we have to think about how to adapt the tool we built in the last chapter. The question is how much we need to change.

Do we need a new machine? Or can we reuse the one we already have? Let's see it with a small example.

<Figure label="the same counting, a longer key"><CountingPairs /></Figure>

If you watch step by step, the counting mechanic is identical. The only thing changing here is that now we record **pairs** instead of single letters. That looks like a minor detail, but it has an enormous impact on the size of what we're building. Let's see it on a real Shakespeare text and watch how the table grows, starting with just the letter "a".

<Figure label="adding rows, one letter at a time"><RowSummer /></Figure>

That number doesn't come out of nowhere. To understand where it comes from, let's set the whole table aside and look at a much smaller part of it.

<Figure label="the same size as the bigram"><SplitTheRow /></Figure>

Put one table next to the other. This whole block takes up exactly the same space as our old bigram model. It's as if each letter needed its own bigram; and since there are 27 possible letters, we need 27 copies. That's why the table grows like this.

<Lead>And what happens if we keep widening the window?</Lead>

<Figure label="one more letter"><GrowingTable /></Figure>

Raising the window size to N=3 or N=4, all we're doing is adding more rows to the same structure. The model we designed in the first chapter wasn't useless; it simply lacked the room to hold a little more context. We've confirmed the architecture scales. Let's see whether it really writes better and is worth it.
</Section>

<Break />

<Section id="ngram-03" number="03" label="Generation" heading="Generation">

We already have our table full of counts and percentages drawn from Shakespeare. Now comes the interesting part: using that information to write new text. How does it do it, exactly — a machine that only knows how to read numbers off a table?

<Figure label="writing is reading a number and rolling a die"><WriteFromMatrix /></Figure>

If the interface looked familiar, it's because you're seeing exactly the same process as before: we look up the current pair in our big table, turn its counts into percentages, and roll the virtual die to pick the next letter. The loop starts again with no new rule.

And since this same logic works for any window size, we can set several models racing at once and really see the difference.

<Figure label="the n-gram race"><LookWhatYouBuilt /></Figure>

If you think about it coldly, it's pretty astonishing. We started literally from nothing: a blank machine that didn't know a single rule of grammar and that, on pure statistics and counting letters, has ended up writing sentences that fool the eye. It isn't that the machine is thinking — but it certainly works.

<Expandable title="The man who fired the linguists" kicker="History · long read · optional">
Before anyone seriously built a machine like yours, the most famous linguist on the planet had already ruled it impossible. Noam Chomsky wrote in 1957 that statistics had nothing interesting to say about language, and years later he drove the nail in with one lapidary line: "the probability of a sentence is an entirely useless notion." His argument was elegant: language is infinite. Almost everything you'll say tomorrow has never been said by anyone. To prove it, he invented a sentence no book had ever contained —"colorless green ideas sleep furiously"—, perfectly grammatical and counting exactly zero in any table. Hold on to that idea, because it comes back.

The man who ended up proving him wrong wasn't a linguist; he didn't even speak good English. Fred Jelinek was born in Czechoslovakia, lost his father in a Nazi concentration camp, and arrived in New York in 1949, sixteen years old, with a refugee's English. He wanted to be a lawyer; the language wasn't there, so he became an engineer. Savor the irony: the kid who couldn't master English ended up spending his life teaching it to machines.

He trained at MIT, in Claude Shannon's circle —the engineer from the previous chapter's expandable—, and inherited his way of looking at the world: speech isn't grammar, it's a noisy signal, and decoding it is a matter of betting. In 1972, IBM put him in charge of a problem that still sounded like science fiction: getting a computer to understand a person talking.

The whole field attacked it with rules: phonetic rules, grammar rules, committees of experts describing the language piece by piece. Jelinek did the opposite. He threw out the rules, brought in counting tables —trigrams, first cousins of yours— and let pure statistics run the show. The more transcription the system swallowed, the better it recognized. Hence the line he went down in history for: "every time I fire a linguist, my system works better." He always swore he never fired anyone; the truth, he said, was just that every time a linguist left the group, the recognizer improved on its own. Which is almost worse.

One of his own, Bob Mercer, compressed the doctrine in 1985 into a motto that governed the field for decades: "there's no data like more data." When the model got it wrong, the answer was almost never a cleverer idea — it was another truckload of text. And it worked, because every new n-gram filled in one more cell of the table.

In the nineties, the same team decided to translate French without knowing any French. Their training material: the proceedings of the Canadian parliament, which by law are published in full in both English and French. Millions of already-translated sentences, for free. The system didn't know what a verb was; it only knew that "la maison" almost always landed where the English said "the house". Professional translators took it as an insult. Within a few years it was the best machine translation in the world, and it's the one Google Translate launched with in 2006.

And you carried it in your pocket without knowing it. T9 on numeric-keypad phones —that little magic of the phone completing the word before you finished— was an n-gram table, a younger sibling of the one you just built.

The final twist sounds made up, but it's true. In 1993, Mercer and his colleague Peter Brown left IBM for a secretive investment fund called Renaissance, to run the same trick on the markets: betting on the next price the way they used to bet on the next word. They became absurdly rich. The counting tables had conquered speech, translation, your pocket, and Wall Street.

The one thing they never conquered was Chomsky's objection. In 2007, Google trained an n-gram on two trillion words; the paper was already titled, fifteen years before ChatGPT, "Large Language Models". And the wall stood where it always had: to improve, you had to remember more context, and remembering more context grew the table faster than all the text in the world could fill it. The linguists lost every battle, but that sentence about colorless green ideas was never actually refuted — it just got buried under mountains of data. You're about to dig it back up.
</Expandable>

Seeing such an obvious improvement from a bigram to a six-letter model, intuition tells us the next step. If we want a perfect model, we just have to raise N. With a 40-letter memory window —enough to remember a whole sentence— we should have a system capable of writing long texts without slipping. Let's try to build it.
</Section>

<Break />

<Section id="ngram-04" number="04" label="The price of memory" heading="The price of memory">

To build a model with N=40 we just follow our own rule: take the size of the previous model and multiply it by 27. Let's nudge the dial up little by little.

<Figure label="the table has no bottom"><ExplosionZoom /></Figure>

Exponential growth is relentless. There comes a point where the table is physically impossible to store on any computer on the planet.

<Expandable title="Tokens: words vs letters" kicker="History · long read · optional">
So far we've counted letters, but there's a decision we made without thinking: why letters and not words? For a long time, engineers tried the opposite. Instead of asking "which letter follows these letters?", they built tables of "which word follows these words?". Those units —words, or pieces of words— are called **tokens**.

It has its logic. A word carries a meaning that a lone letter doesn't, so predicting whole words gave much more coherent text up front. The problem shows up the moment you count how many distinct pieces you have to handle.

<Figure label="27 against 50,000"><WordsExplosion /></Figure>

A language like English easily has fifty thousand common words. Our alphabet had twenty-seven symbols; here there are fifty thousand. A table for a letter bigram needs 27 rows; one for words needs 50,000. And going up to a word trigram —fifty thousand by fifty thousand— jumps to two and a half billion rows. The explosion you saw with letters happens far sooner here.

And the table ends up even emptier. The vast majority of those fifty thousand words never appear together: "umbrella theorem" or "howl notary" are cells no text will ever fill. The more units, the more holes.

There's also a cost letters didn't have. The twenty-seven letters work for anything you write: they get reused. Words don't. A table trained on English knows nothing about "perro" or "mañana"; if you want a Spanish model, you have to build a new table from scratch, counting all over again on mountains of Spanish text. Each language, its own giant, half-empty table.

That's why swapping letters for words didn't break the wall: it brought it closer. It gave nicer text at first in exchange for a bigger, emptier table that couldn't be reused. The underlying problem —counting only knows how to repeat what it already saw— stayed intact.
</Expandable>

Fine, size is a problem. But storage always gets cheaper. Let's imagine for a moment that we have infinite hard drives and can store whatever table we want.

And notice what we're asking when we store it. With a forty-letter window —a whole sentence— each row no longer answers "which letter follows *th*"; it answers something far more ambitious: **what comes after this entire sentence**. The key to each row is, practically, a whole sentence.

Even so, even without the physical limit, the method has a far more serious flaw.

<Figure label="lean into the whole table"><EmptyVoid /></Figure>

After reading all the text on the Internet, the vast majority of the table is still empty. And it makes sense that it is: there are letter combinations that simply never go together because they mean nothing. If they never appear in the real world, it seems like we shouldn't care that their cell is a zero. What, exactly, is the problem with the table being empty?

<Lead>Try it yourself: type a perfectly ordinary sentence and watch which row it looks for.</Lead>

<Figure label="a new sentence breaks it"><QuantumElephant /></Figure>

This is the problem with having an empty table. "The quantum elephant" is a strange phrase, but there's nothing wrong with it. Even if you've never heard it before, you could probably make up several reasonable ways to continue it. Our machine can't.

By relying only on exact matches, it needs to have seen that specific combination at some point in its training. When it tries to look it up in the table and finds nothing, it's left with no information to lean on.

And this problem doesn't go away no matter how much data we add. However much text we gather, there will always be new sentences, unexpected combinations, expressions nobody had written before. Language is constantly changing; the table is not. So when the machine runs into something that falls outside what it has memorized, it's left with no answer.

But here an interesting doubt appears. Is the problem really the holes in the table? Or is there something deeper we're missing?
</Section>

<Section id="ngram-05" number="05" label="The end of counting" heading="The end of counting">

The quantum-elephant example showed us what happens when the machine runs into a combination it has never seen. We might think that's the main problem: if the table is empty, of course it doesn't know what to do.

But it turns out the machine keeps failing even when the information it needs does exist in the table. Let's see it with an example far more everyday than the elephant.

<Figure label="the blindness of counting"><BigModelLimit /></Figure>

To us it's obvious that if a dog sleeps, a cat can too. We know they're both animals and share behaviors; we don't need to have read the exact sentence to guess how it continues.

But look at the table. To the model, "el perro" lands in one row and "el gato" in another thousands of rows away. They're sealed boxes, with no bridge between them. The machine couldn't care less that both are animals: to it they're two different strings of letters with nothing to do with one another.

If it hasn't memorized the exact match, it goes blind. It can't generalize, it can't apply logic.

<Lead>It is, literally, a parrot with a very good memory.</Lead>

We've taken the method of counting letters to its physical and logical limit. And although it carried us a long way, this wall is impassable.

If we want to build a true artificial intelligence that can survive sentences it has never seen, we have to abandon the idea of using giant tables. We have to change the paradigm entirely.

Instead of building a machine that counts and memorizes the past, we need to build one that can try to guess, realize it got it wrong, and adjust its internal gears so it doesn't fail again. We need a system that, instead of counting, is able to learn.

And to achieve that, engineers had to stop looking at statistics textbooks and start looking at biology.

<Lead>Welcome to the age of learning.</Lead>
</Section>
