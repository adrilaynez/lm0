<Section id="nn-01" number="01" label="The Spark" heading="Let's Teach a Machine to Learn">

<Lead>Counting hit a wall. <Highlight tooltip="Learning = adjusting weights so the answers get less wrong over time.">N-grams can't share knowledge between similar patterns</Highlight> and their tables explode with every extra character of context. We need a completely different approach — one that doesn't memorize, but discovers.</Lead>

Remember the bigram model? It counted how often one letter follows another. When you typed "th", it looked up how many times each letter appeared after "th" in the training text. But internally, letters are just numbers — 't' might be 19, 'h' might be 7. The bigram model never did any math with those numbers. It just used them as addresses in a giant table.

What if, instead of a lookup table, we actually computed something with those numbers? What if we could take the numbers for 't' and 'h', do some math, and get a prediction for what comes next?

<CountingVsLearning />

That's the core idea of a neural network: it takes numbers, follows a few simple steps, and produces an answer. The only hard part is <Highlight tooltip="Weights decide how much each input matters.">choosing the right steps</Highlight> so the answers are usually correct.

Imagine predicting how long your commute to school takes. You know two things: the distance (8 km) and the traffic level (3 on a scale of 1–10). With just those two numbers, how could you make a prediction for travel time?

<Callout title="Inputs are data, weights are learnable">
Here's something crucial: the distance and traffic level are measurements — you can't change them. The only things you CAN change are the weights. This is the fundamental rule of neural networks: inputs are fixed data, weights are the knobs the model learns to turn.
</Callout>

Start simple: take 8 (distance) and 3 (traffic). Try a few ways to combine them and watch how the result changes.

Now those two numbers represent real measurements of your daily commute. The weighted sum gives you a predicted travel time in minutes. By adjusting the weights, you control how much distance and traffic each contribute to the prediction.

A negative weight would mean more distance somehow reduces time (physically nonsensical — but the model doesn't know physics, it just follows the math).

By turning weights into volume knobs, you can make one factor drown out the other — exactly what a neuron does when it learns what matters." />

One more tiny upgrade. What if both measurements are zero — you live at school and there's no traffic — but you still need 5 minutes to walk out the door? Or the opposite: even with a decent commute, you always arrive early. That 'default offset' is a bias — a constant push that shifts the predicted time up or down.

<Predict>If both inputs are zero, what should the output be? What if you want a non-zero default?</Predict>

<Callout title="You just built a neuron">
Weighted sum + bias. That's it. Every neural network in the world — from the simplest to GPT-4 — is built from units doing exactly this. The rest is scale and clever architecture.
</Callout>

You now have a single computing unit: inputs × weights + bias = output. But right now it's just a formula. Let's put all the pieces together and see it work.

<KeyTakeaway>
A <Term word="neuron">neuron</Term> takes inputs, multiplies each by a <Term word="weight">weight</Term>, adds a <Term word="bias">bias</Term>, and outputs a number. These three ingredients are all you need to start learning.
</KeyTakeaway>

<HiddenSection category="historical" difficulty={1} title="Biology vs Machine" description="Inspired by nature, but vastly simpler">
The artificial neuron was directly inspired by how real brain cells work. McCulloch and Pitts studied biological neurons in 1943 and noticed a pattern: signals arrive through dendrites, get processed in the cell body, and if the total signal is strong enough, the neuron fires an output down the axon. They asked: can we build a mathematical model that does the same thing?

<BiologicalVsArtificialDiagram />

The resemblance is intentional but shallow. Real neurons are complex electrochemical systems with timing, inhibition, and plasticity. Artificial neurons are a mathematical simplification that captures just one idea: signals in, computation, signal out.
</HiddenSection>

Here's what happens inside: the neuron multiplies each input by its weight (to control importance), adds them together, adds the bias (a starting push), and finally passes the result through a simple filter called an activation function. ReLU is the simplest: if the number is positive, it passes through unchanged. If it's negative, it becomes zero. Think of it as a gate that only lets positive signals through.

With the right weights and bias, a single neuron can <Highlight tooltip="An activation is the non-linear 'gate' that lets neural nets bend lines into curves.">draw a straight line through data and classify everything on one side as A and the other as B</Highlight>. Training is how the neuron finds those right numbers — starting from random guesses and improving step by step.

<Callout title="What are parameters?">
Weights and biases together are called parameters. At the start, they're random noise. By the end, they encode everything the network has learned — stored as nothing more than a list of decimal numbers.
</Callout>

In math, everything you just explored can be written in one line:

<FormulaBlock formula="y = f\left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i \, x_i + b\right)" caption="Multiply each input by its weight, sum everything, add the bias, then pass it through an activation function f. This is the atomic operation of every neural network." />

<HistorySidebar />

One neuron can compute a weighted sum and fire (or not). That's powerful — but also limited. What happens when we put many of these units together?

<Section id="nn-03" number="03" label="Scaling Up" heading="What If We Add More Neurons?">

<Lead>One neuron can only draw one straight line. What happens when we combine several? Let's find out — step by step.</Lead>

Now let's add more neurons. Instead of a single neuron producing a single output, put several side by side — all looking at the same inputs, each with its own weights and bias.

{/* ParallelNeuronsDemo — several neurons looking at the same inputs, each detecting its own pattern: one output becomes many, and that is a layer. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Interactive · Parallel Neurons" hint="Add neurons and toggle ReLU to see how multiple neurons process the same inputs independently."><ParallelNeuronsDemo /></Figure>

Each neuron learns to detect a different pattern in the same data. This is called a layer — a group of neurons working in parallel. One output becomes many.

But what does each neuron actually do? Think of it as asking one yes-or-no question: is this data point above or below my line? The neuron computes its weighted sum, and the result is either positive (yes) or negative (no). That dividing line is the neuron's decision boundary.

For example: 'Is the commute time more than 30 minutes?' One neuron, one question, one straight line splitting the space in two. Multiple neurons ask multiple questions — and by combining their answers, we can describe much more complex regions.

Here's what that looks like in practice. A single neuron draws one straight line through the data and says "everything on this side is A, everything on that side is B." Try it — drag the weights to move the line:

{/* DecisionBoundaryIntro — one neuron is a single straight line: it splits the simple case, but the reader hits the wall that XOR can't be cut with one line. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Interactive · Decision Boundary" hint="Drag the weight sliders to move the decision line. Try both scenarios — simple and XOR."><DecisionBoundaryIntro /></Figure>

Start with the simple scenario — one line is enough. Then switch to XOR mode and try to reach 100% accuracy. You'll find it's impossible with a single neuron.

<Peak>This stumped the field for twenty years.</Peak>

<Reflection>One neuron, one line. Two neurons, two lines. What happens with a hundred?</Reflection>

XOR showed one layer isn't enough. What if we stack layers — one after another — so the output of one becomes the input of the next?

Here's the first surprise: if you just stack layers of neurons without anything special between them, you get nothing new. <Highlight color="amber" tooltip="Non-linearity = the reason deep nets can do more than draw straight lines.">Two layers collapse into one. Ten layers collapse into one.</Highlight> It's like stacking magnifying glasses — they just combine into a single magnification. Try it:

Add layers below, then toggle the activation function to see the difference.

{/* LinearStackingDemo — stacking layers without activation is an illusion of depth: ten linear layers collapse into a single straight line. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Interactive · Stacking Layers" hint="Add layers and watch: without an activation function, the combined effect is always a straight line. With ReLU, every layer adds a bend."><LinearStackingDemo /></Figure>

Without activation functions, depth is an illusion. The network is still just one big linear operation — no matter how many layers you add.

<HiddenSection category="math" difficulty={2} title="What if you stacked 100 layers without activation?" description="A proof using matrix algebra that any number of linear layers collapses into one.">
Each linear layer is just a matrix multiplication: output = W · input. Two layers? That's W₂ · (W₁ · input) = (W₂ · W₁) · input. The product of two matrices is just another matrix. So two layers = one layer. A hundred layers? Still one matrix.

<Math>{`\\underset{\\text{Layer 2}}{W_2} \\cdot \\underset{\\text{Layer 1}}{W_1} = \\underset{\\text{Single equivalent}}{W_3}`}</Math>

<Math>{`\\begin{bmatrix} a & b \\\\ c & d \\end{bmatrix} \\cdot \\begin{bmatrix} e & f \\\\ g & h \\end{bmatrix} = \\begin{bmatrix} ae+bg & af+bh \\\\ ce+dg & cf+dh \\end{bmatrix}`}</Math>

No matter how many layers you stack, without non-linearity between them, the entire network collapses into a single matrix multiplication. Depth is an illusion. This is the linear collapse problem — and it's why activation functions are non-negotiable.
</HiddenSection>

The fix is a single small idea: after each neuron's sum, apply a non-linear function. This breaks the collapse. Every layer now adds a genuine bend. Explore the most common activation functions below:

{/* NNActivationExplorer — the spark that breaks the collapse: each activation bends the line a different way, and comparing their personalities is the point. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Interactive · Activation Functions" hint="Click each function to compare shapes. ReLU is the default for modern networks — simple but effective."><NNActivationExplorer /></Figure>

Different functions have different personalities. <Highlight color="indigo" tooltip="ReLU = max(0, x). It keeps positive signals and kills negative ones.">ReLU passes positives unchanged and kills negatives. Sigmoid squashes everything into 0–1. Tanh squashes into −1 to 1.</Highlight> The choice matters: ReLU made deep networks practical by avoiding a training problem called vanishing gradients, where error signals shrink to near-zero in early layers.

Now for the payoff. Remember the XOR problem that one neuron couldn't solve? With two neurons and ReLU activation, the network can draw two boundary lines — creating a band that separates the classes. Toggle between the modes to see the difference:

{/* XORSolverDemo — the payoff: two neurons with ReLU solve the XOR a single one never could; simple pieces plus non-linearity beat the complex. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Interactive · XOR Solved with ReLU" hint="Toggle between linear (no activation) and ReLU to see how two neurons with activation can solve the XOR problem."><XORSolverDemo /></Figure>

Two neurons with ReLU solved what one neuron never could. Each hidden neuron draws one line; the output neuron combines their answers. This is the fundamental power of neural networks: simple pieces, combined with non-linearity, can solve complex problems.

<Callout title="The recipe so far">
Take neurons (weighted sum + bias). Add activation functions to unlock non-linearity. Put neurons in parallel to get a layer. Stack layers in series to get depth. That's the architecture of every neural network.
</Callout>

<Peak>There HAS to be a better way.</Peak>

What if we just try changing a weight and see what happens? Use the sliders to adjust w₁ and w₂. Try to make the output equal 3.

{/* NudgeWeightDemo — each weight has its own leverage: nudging it moves the output in proportion to its input, the first intuition for sensitivity (the derivative). (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="What If We Nudge a Weight?" hint="Drag the weight sliders and watch the output change. Notice which weight has more effect."><NudgeWeightDemo /></Figure>

Did you notice? Changing w₁ by 1 moves the output by 1 (because x₁ = 1). Changing w₂ by 1 moves the output by 2 (because x₂ = 2). Each weight has a different leverage. But how do we measure that leverage precisely — for any operation, not just this one?

That worked for one operation. But our neuron chains MANY operations — multiply, add, activate. We need a way to measure the effect of a change through an entire chain. Start with the simplest case:

{/* DerivativeIntuitionDemo — the derivative without formulas: in a sum the effect is always 1, in a multiplication it depends on the other number. Measuring how much one thing affects another. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Measuring Sensitivity" hint="Toggle between addition and multiplication. Change x and y to see how the result changes."><DerivativeIntuitionDemo /></Figure>

For addition, the derivative is always 1. For multiplication (x × y), the derivative of x is y — the effect of x depends on how big y is. This simple idea — measuring how much one thing affects another — is the foundation of how neural networks learn. Now: what happens when operations are chained?

In a real neuron, operations are chained: multiply by weight, then add bias. A change at the input ripples through every step. Watch it:

{/* ChainRuleBuilder — the chain rule made visible: a change at the input ripples step by step, and the local effects multiply to give the total effect. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Chaining Operations" hint="Move the x slider and watch how the change propagates through each operation."><ChainRuleBuilder /></Figure>

To find the total effect, multiply the individual effects along the chain. This is the chain rule — and it works no matter how many operations you stack. A network with millions of operations? Same idea, applied backwards from the output all the way to the first weight. Now we know the size of the effect. But which direction should we move the weight?

<Section id="nn-05" number="05" label="Training" heading="Making It Learn">

<Lead>You know the direction. You know the size. Now let's put it all together — compute the loss, follow the gradient, and watch the weights improve step by step.</Lead>

The output is 10, we wanted 3, so the error is 7. But raw errors have a problem: positives and negatives can cancel each other out across many examples. We need something better.

<HiddenSection category="math" difficulty={2} title="Why Square the Error?" description="See why raw errors are problematic and how squaring fixes it.">
<LossFormulaMotivation />
</HiddenSection>

Squaring the error gives us the loss — a single positive number that measures how wrong the model is. The bigger the loss, the worse the prediction. Now we have everything: the direction to move (gradient), the size of each step (learning rate), and a score to minimize (loss). Let's put it all together.

Now that we know how to measure error, we can ask the key question: if we change one weight slightly, does the loss go up or down? Let's trace through a real neuron to find out. The chain is simple: input × weight + bias → activation function → output → compare to target → loss.

{/* WeightImpactVisualizer — moving one weight ripples through the whole neuron to the loss: the reader sees, without graphs, that a weight nudge shrinks or grows the error. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Interactive · How a Weight Changes the Loss" hint="Drag the weight slider and watch the entire chain update: input × weight + bias → σ → output → loss. Then see what happens if you nudge the weight by 0.1."><WeightImpactVisualizer /></Figure>

You just saw it with your own eyes: nudging the weight by a tiny amount changes the loss. If the loss went up, we should move the weight the other direction. If the loss went down, we should keep going. This idea — 'which direction makes the loss smaller?' — is exactly what the derivative captures. Let's now see this as a graph:

The same idea we just explored — nudging a weight and checking if the loss goes up or down — can be visualized as a curve. Every possible weight value has a corresponding loss. The derivative at any point is the slope of this curve: steep slope means big effect, flat slope means little effect.

{/* LossDerivativeVisualizer — the same idea as a curve: every weight has its loss, and the slope of the tangent IS the derivative telling you how much the error changes. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Interactive · The Derivative as a Graph" hint="Drag the weight and see the loss curve. The slope of the tangent line IS the derivative — it tells you which direction to move."><LossDerivativeVisualizer /></Figure>

We know the loss — now which direction should we move each weight to reduce it? The slope of the loss curve tells us:

{/* LossWeightParabolaVisualizer — the sign of the slope is the compass: it tells which way to move the weight to descend toward the bottom of the loss bowl. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Loss Landscape" hint="Drag the point along the curve to see how the slope changes. The slope tells the model which way to move."><LossWeightParabolaVisualizer /></Figure>

Always move opposite to the direction that makes things worse. If the derivative is positive and the output is too high — decrease the weight. If the output is too low — increase it. This one rule is behind all of neural network training. But we still need a single number that captures how wrong the model is — so we can track progress.

One complete training step: compute the output, measure the loss, calculate the gradients, update the weights. Five phases. Watch them happen:

{/* NeuronGradientCalculator — one full training step end to end: forward, loss, gradients backward, and update — backpropagation done by hand. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="One Complete Training Step" hint="Step through the five phases of a single training iteration."><NeuronGradientCalculator /></Figure>

What do those gradient numbers mean? If the gradient for w₁ is +14, it means: increasing w₁ by 1 would increase the loss by 14. That's bad — so we decrease w₁ instead. The sign tells you the direction; the size tells you how sensitive the loss is to that weight.

Let's pause and give names to everything you just learned.

<Callout title="Naming what you just learned">
Computing the output is the forward pass. Squaring the error gives us the loss. Computing how each weight affects the loss gives us the gradients (the backward pass). Updating weights by subtracting the gradient is gradient descent. The full loop — forward, loss, backward, update — is called backpropagation. You just did it by hand!
</Callout>

Now that you understand gradients, here's a catch with ReLU: if a neuron's pre-activation is always negative, ReLU clips it to zero — and zero gradient means zero learning. The neuron is permanently dead. This is the 'dying ReLU' problem.

<HiddenSection category="advanced" difficulty={2} title="Advanced: What If the Derivative Is Zero?" description="The vanishing gradient problem — why some neurons stop learning entirely.">
If the derivative is zero, the weight gets zero update. No learning happens. This isn't just a theoretical concern — it's one of the most important problems in deep learning. Let's see exactly when and why this happens with the sigmoid function.

{/* FlatGradientVisualizer — far from the center the sigmoid flattens: its derivative drops near zero, the neuron saturates and learning stalls. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Interactive · Sigmoid Saturation Zones" hint="Drag the input value. In the red zones, the derivative is nearly zero — the neuron is 'saturated' and can't learn."><FlatGradientVisualizer /></Figure>

You saw that the sigmoid derivative goes to zero in the saturation zones. But what does that actually mean for learning? Let's trace the full backpropagation chain. During training, the gradient for each weight is computed by multiplying several terms together. If ANY one of those terms is zero, the entire gradient is zero — and the weight doesn't change at all.

{/* BackpropZeroDemo — the gradient is a product of chained terms: if a single one (a saturated σ'(z)) goes to zero, the whole gradient dies and the weight doesn't move. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Interactive · Backpropagation with Zero Derivative" hint="Move z into the saturation zones (far from 0) and watch σ'(z) collapse to zero — killing the entire gradient chain. The weight update becomes zero: the neuron stops learning."><BackpropZeroDemo /></Figure>

<Math>{`\\sigma'(z) = \\sigma(z)(1 - \\sigma(z))`}</Math>

When z is very large or very negative, σ(z) is close to 0 or 1. Then σ(z)(1 − σ(z)) ≈ 0 × 1 = 0 or 1 × 0 = 0. The derivative vanishes. In a deep network, these tiny derivatives multiply together layer by layer — 0.01 × 0.01 × 0.01 = 0.000001. The gradient signal dies before reaching early layers.

This is why ReLU replaced sigmoid in deep networks. ReLU's derivative is exactly 1 for positive inputs — the gradient flows unchanged. Modern architectures also use batch normalization, residual connections, and careful initialization to keep gradients alive.
</HiddenSection>

<Reflection>Each step is tiny. But thousands of tiny steps in the right direction can solve problems no human could program by hand.</Reflection>

<KeyTakeaway>
<Term word="gradient descent">Gradient descent</Term> is how neural networks learn: measure the <Term word="loss">loss</Term>, compute the <Term word="gradient">gradient</Term>, and nudge each <Term word="weight">weight</Term> in the direction that reduces error. Repeat thousands of times.
</KeyTakeaway>

<Section id="nn-06" number="06" label="Scale" heading="Training at Scale">

<Lead>One neuron, one example, one step. That's the toy version. Real training processes thousands of examples in batches, repeats for many epochs, and must avoid a dangerous trap: overfitting.</Lead>

Before we go further, let's clarify some terminology you'll see everywhere in machine learning.

<TermCards />

{/* StepEpochBatchCounter — pulls apart three words that get confused: a batch is a group of examples, a step one update, an epoch a full pass over the data. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Steps, Epochs & Batches" hint="Watch data flow through the network in batches. One full pass through all data = one epoch."><StepEpochBatchCounter /></Figure>

You've seen how one example flows through the network. But real datasets have thousands or millions of examples. Training on them one-by-one would take forever. The solution: batches.

In practice, computing gradients one example at a time is inefficient. Modern GPUs can process hundreds of examples in parallel. Instead of updating weights after every single example, we average the gradients from a small batch of examples — typically 32 to 256 — and update once per batch. This is called mini-batch gradient descent.

Batch size controls a fundamental trade-off. A batch size of 1 (stochastic gradient descent, or SGD) gives noisy gradients — each update points in a slightly different direction because it's based on one random example. A batch size equal to the full dataset gives perfectly smooth gradients, but it's slow and can overfit. Mini-batches strike a balance: reasonably stable gradients with efficient computation.

<Callout title="Why noise helps">
Gradient noise isn't just a necessary evil — it's a feature. Small batches introduce randomness that helps the optimizer explore the loss landscape more thoroughly, escaping shallow local minima and finding solutions that generalize better to new data.
</Callout>

Modern deep learning standardizes on mini-batches of 32–256 examples. Larger batches train faster per epoch but may generalize worse. Smaller batches are noisier but often find better solutions.

{/* GradientNoiseVisualizer — the batch decides how noisy the gradient is: few examples give scattered arrows, many a single clean arrow toward the true descent. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Gradient Noise vs Batch Size" hint="Larger batches give smoother gradients but each step processes more data. Smaller batches are noisier but update weights more frequently per epoch. Compare the curves to see the trade-off."><GradientNoiseVisualizer /></Figure>

Why not just train on one example at a time? Imagine you're trying to learn the average height of 12 people. If you only look at one person per step, a very tall person will wildly skew your estimate. Let's watch all 3 batch sizes train side by side — same data, same learning rate, different batch sizes:

{/* BatchSizeComparisonVisualizer — three batch sizes running at once: with 1 the path is erratic, with all it's clean; the noise-stability trade-off in one glance. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Interactive · Why Batch Size Matters" hint="Watch all 3 batch sizes train simultaneously. Red (batch=1) is noisy and chaotic; yellow (batch=4) is smoother; green (batch=ALL) is a clean path to the answer."><BatchSizeComparisonVisualizer /></Figure>

This instability with small batches is a preview of a deeper problem: if your training data isn't representative, your model will learn the wrong patterns entirely. This is called overfitting — and it gets its own section next.

<Section id="nn-07" number="07" label="Overfitting" heading="The Risk of Overfitting">

<Lead>A network that trains perfectly on every example can still fail completely on new data. It memorized instead of learned. This is overfitting — one of the most important concepts in all of machine learning.</Lead>

{/* OverfittingPlayground — three models on the same data; testing on new points reveals only the middle one generalizes, the others memorized or fell short. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="The Overfitting Playground" hint="Three models, same data. Only one generalizes well to new, unseen points. Click 'Test' to find out which."><OverfittingPlayground /></Figure>

When a network trains, loss decreases and predictions improve. The natural instinct is to keep going — train longer, push loss as low as possible. But this leads to a trap: the network can achieve near-zero error on the training examples while learning nothing general. It memorizes the specific patterns in the data it saw, including the noise and quirks, and becomes useless on new examples.

Think of a student preparing for an exam. One student memorizes every answer to every practice problem without understanding the underlying concepts. Another student learns the principles and can apply them to new problems. On the practice test, both score perfectly. On the real exam with different questions, only the second student succeeds. The first student overfit to the practice set.

The solution is a train/validation split. Hold out a portion of the data — say 20% — that the model never sees during training. Measure performance on both the training set (data the model learns from) and the validation set (held-out data). Training loss always decreases as the model gets better at fitting what it sees. Validation loss follows a U-curve: it improves as the model learns real patterns, then worsens as the model starts memorizing training-specific noise.

{/* TrainValSplitVisualizer — the defense against overfitting: set aside a slice of data the model never sees, so it can be judged honestly. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Interactive · Train / Validation Split" hint="Click 'Split' to see how data is divided into training and validation sets. The model only trains on the filled dots."><TrainValSplitVisualizer /></Figure>

<Callout title="Why we can't just minimize training loss">
Driving training loss to zero is easy — just memorize every training example. But the goal isn't to ace a test you've already seen. It's to predict patterns you've never encountered. Validation loss is the only honest measure of whether a model has learned something real.
</Callout>

{/* OverfittingComparisonDiagram — zero training error is not a good sign: the wiggly curve touching every point fails on new data; the smooth one gets it right. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Comparison · Good Fit vs Overfit" hint="Same training data, two different models. The overfit model achieves zero training error by fitting a complex curve through every point. But on new test data (hollow circles), it fails. The good-fit model accepts some training error but generalizes correctly."><OverfittingComparisonDiagram /></Figure>

{/* TrainValLossCurveVisualizer — training loss always falls, but validation loss traces a U: its lowest point marks when to stop before memorizing kicks in. (Mechanics: see the .tsx) */}
<Figure label="Interactive · Train vs Validation Loss Over Time" hint="Drag the epoch slider to see how training and validation loss evolve. Training loss always decreases. Validation loss forms a U-curve — improving, then worsening as overfitting begins. The optimal stopping point is where validation loss is lowest."><TrainValLossCurveVisualizer /></Figure>

Model complexity amplifies this trade-off. A small model struggles to fit even the training data (underfitting). A right-sized model fits the training data and generalizes to new data. A huge model with far more parameters than needed memorizes the training set perfectly but fails on validation data (overfitting). More capacity is not always better.

How do we fight overfitting? More training data dilutes the memorization effect. Regularization techniques penalize model complexity. Early stopping halts training when validation loss stops improving. Dropout, data augmentation, and BatchNorm all help. The key insight: validation loss is the only honest metric. A model can lie to you with perfect training performance while being completely useless in practice.

<Callout title="The generalization test">
A model that performs well on training data but poorly on validation data has failed the generalization test. It learned the noise, not the signal. In ML, the validation set is the ultimate judge — it's the only data the model hasn't seen and can't cheat on.
</Callout>

<Reflection>The gap between training loss and validation loss is the gap between memorizing and understanding.</Reflection>

Overfitting is why we split data, why we watch validation metrics closely, and why bigger models aren't always better. Every ML practitioner learns this lesson — usually by watching a model train to 'perfect' performance and then fail spectacularly on real-world data. The MLP explorer you'll see next flags overfitting automatically, but now you know what it means and why it matters.

<KeyTakeaway>
<Term word="overfitting">Overfitting</Term> happens when a model memorizes training data instead of learning patterns. Split your data into train and validation sets, and watch the <Term word="validation loss">validation loss</Term> to know when to stop.
</KeyTakeaway>

Softmax gives us probabilities. But we trained this network — how did we measure how WRONG it was? We need a loss function designed for probability outputs.

Cross-entropy has an elegant simplicity: it only looks at the probability assigned to the correct answer. Why the logarithm? Because log converts 'halving the probability' into 'adding a fixed penalty' — and the lower the probability, the more harshly log punishes. Give the right answer a 1% probability and the loss is 4.6. Give it 50% and the loss is only 0.7. That steep curve is exactly the pressure the model needs to get confident fast.

Now we have the full picture: softmax turns raw scores into probabilities, and cross-entropy measures the error by asking one question — how much probability did you give the right answer?

<HiddenSection category="math" difficulty={2} title="Math: Why softmax(Wx) Recovers the Bigram Table" description="A beautiful result: the optimal 27×27 weight matrix W exactly encodes log-bigram-probabilities.">
Here's the remarkable mathematical connection. Our network computes softmax(W·x) where W is a 27×27 matrix and x is a one-hot vector selecting a row. The probability of character j following character i is:

<Math>{`P(\\text{next} = j \\mid \\text{prev} = i) = \\frac{e^{W_{ij}}}{\\sum_k e^{W_{ik}}}`}</Math>

When x is a one-hot vector for character i, the matrix product W·x simply selects row i of W. So Wᵢⱼ is the logit (raw score) for character j given input i. After training, the optimal Wᵢⱼ converges to log P(j|i) — the log of the bigram probability. Softmax then exponentiates and normalizes, recovering the exact bigram distribution.

<Math>{`\\mathbf{W} \\in \\mathbb{R}^{27 \\times 27}`}</Math>

This is why the neural network and the counting table give the same answer: with 27 inputs and 27 outputs, the weight matrix has exactly 27×27 = 729 parameters — the same number of entries as the bigram table. The architecture constrains the network to learn nothing more and nothing less than the bigram distribution.
</HiddenSection>
